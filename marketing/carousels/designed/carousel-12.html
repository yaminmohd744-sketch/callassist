7 things a decade in sales taught me about people.

None of these are in any business book.

Everyone is selling something.

Every conversation is an exchange of value. Your job interview, your first date, the argument with your landlord, the text asking for a favour. When you understand this, you stop being passive in conversations and start thinking about what the other person needs.

Start thinking about what the other person needs to hear — and why.

Rejection is just redirection
wearing an unfamiliar coat.

The company that didn't hire you. The relationship that ended. The "no, not right now" on a proposal you believed in. In sales you hear enough of them that you stop needing them to be personal.

Stop needing them to be personal.

03

Confidence is a skill.
Not a personality type.

Before my first call I was terrified. After 500 calls I was comfortable. After 2,000 I was confident. Confidence comes from repetition, from feedback, and from surviving things you thought would break you.

By doing the thing anyway.

Listening is the most
underrated superpower.

The person who asks the best questions controls every conversation — not because they're manipulating, but because they're genuinely learning what the other person cares about. In sales it closes deals. Everywhere else, it builds trust faster than anything you can say.

The person who asks the best questions
controls every conversation.

05

Timing is everything —
and you can learn to read it.

Sales taught me that the right offer at the wrong time is a no. And that a mediocre offer at exactly the right moment wins. People buy when they're in pain, when timing aligns, and when trust exists. You can't force the timing — but you can learn to read the signals.

The right offer at the wrong time is a no.

6 / 10

06

Persistence beats talent
more often than anyone admits.

I've watched brilliant communicators burn out because they couldn't handle the grind. And I've watched average communicators grind their way to VP. The deciding variable is almost never raw talent.

Consistency and the willingness to improve every week — regardless of results.

Your network isn't your net worth. But it's close.

The jobs I got. The deals that moved. The introductions that changed things. Almost all of it came through people, not portals. Sales teaches you to build relationships intentionally — to give before you ask, to show up without an agenda.

Every conversation like it matters.
Because eventually one of them will.

The skill that transfers everywhere.

Communication. The ability to understand what someone needs, frame your message around it, handle uncertainty, and ask for what you want directly. Sales doesn't just teach you to sell products.

Sell yourself,
your ideas,
your vision.
