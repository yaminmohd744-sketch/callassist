Listening isn't passive. It's the most active part of selling.

When a rep goes quiet, most prospects assume the rep is thinking about what to say next. The top 1% are doing something else entirely — they're decoding. Finding the signal in the noise.

"The real answers are in what's said between the answers."

Signal Type 1: Budget

Budget signals — what to listen for.

Direct

"We have budget approved."

Indirect

"We had a similar tool last year but the cost came up during renewal."

Buried (easiest to miss)

"Our CFO is very focused on ROI this quarter."

Signal Type 2: Timeline

Timeline signals — what to listen for.

Active timeline

"We need to have something in place before Q3."

Delayed pain

"We've been looking at this for a while."

Hidden deadline

"I have a board review in 6 weeks."

When you hear timeline language, you've found the hook for every follow-up.

Pitchr

Signal Type 3: Authority

Authority signals — who actually decides.

Not the decision-maker

"I'll need to loop in my manager."

Influence, not authority

"My team would need to be on board."

Champion + closer in one

"I can move on this."

Map the org before you pitch.

Signal Type 4: Pain Intensity

How much does it actually hurt?

Low — "It's a bit of an issue."

Medium — "It's costing us time every week."

High — "We brought in a consultant last month."

The intensity of the pain predicts the speed of the sale.

Signal Type 5: Competitor Mentions

Competitor signals — what they reveal.

Live evaluation

"We're also looking at [Competitor]."

Previous failure

"We tried [Competitor] before."

Switching cost mindset

"Our current provider is [Competitor]."

These change your entire approach.

The easiest way to get better at listening.

After every call, write down three things the prospect said that you didn't fully explore. Not the objections — the off-hand comments. The "well, ideally…" statements. The "we've been meaning to…" clauses.

These are the threads you pull in the follow-up.

That's where the deal actually lives.
