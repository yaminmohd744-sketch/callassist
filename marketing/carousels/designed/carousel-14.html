The moment before you dial.

You know the feeling. You have the number. You have the script. And you just sit there for three seconds. Maybe five. The tab stays open. The phone is in your hand. And something in your chest says "not yet."

This isn't weakness.

It's your nervous system doing exactly what it's designed to do when it senses social risk.

It has a name.

Call reluctance.

Research by Dudley & Goodson identified 12 distinct types. It's not correlated with intelligence, work ethic, or desire to succeed. It's a specific learned response to anticipated social rejection.

What the research actually says about rejection.

The brain processes social rejection in the same neural pathway as physical pain.

A cold call hang-up activates the same region as being physically hurt. You're not being dramatic.

Your brain is doing exactly what it evolved to do — in a world where the stakes of a dial were much higher than voicemail.

How to rewire your
relationship with the dial.

Don't set outcome goals for the first 20 calls of a session. Set process goals: “My goal is to open well and ask one good question.”

Remove the result from the equation.

When your brain learns that a dial doesn't require a yes to be a success, the anticipation anxiety drops dramatically.

You're retraining the threat model.
