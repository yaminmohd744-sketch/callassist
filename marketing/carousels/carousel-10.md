## Slide 2
**Headline:** The core finding: losses feel 2x heavier than equivalent gains.
**Body copy:** Kahneman's prospect theory shows that losing $100 feels psychologically twice as painful as gaining $100 feels good. Applied to sales: when a buyer hears your pitch, they don't just evaluate what they gain. They evaluate what they might lose — money, status, time, the ability to reverse the decision.

**Visual direction:**
- Background: #0a0a0a
- Scale illustration — left side weighs "Gain $100" (lighter, #814ac8), right side weighs "Lose $100" (heavier, #ff4444, visually heavier on the scale)
- The scale tips toward the loss side
- Body copy at 16px in #c4b8e0 below the illustration
- "Losses are weighted 2:1 over gains." in #df7afe bold

## Slide 3
**Headline:** This is why "you'll gain X" doesn't close deals.
**Body copy:** "You'll gain 3 hours a week per rep" — nice, but easy to dismiss. The gain is in the future, abstract, uncertain. "Right now you're losing 3 hours a week per rep to manual logging" — that's a present, concrete, felt pain. Frame your value in terms of what they're currently losing, not what they stand to gain.

**Reframe:** From "You'll get X" → To "Right now you're losing X."

**Visual direction:**
- Background: #111111
- Two-panel card: "Gain framing" (grey, muted) vs "Loss framing" (#814ac8, vivid) — each with example copy inside
- Arrow pointing from the grey card to the purple card — the shift you want them to make
- "Reframe:" callout below in #df7afe

## Slide 4
**Headline:** Status quo bias: why doing nothing feels safe.
**Body copy:** Kahneman identified that people have a systematic bias toward the current state. Changing feels risky. Staying feels safe — even when staying is clearly more costly. Your prospect isn't irrational. Their brain is doing exactly what it evolved to do: protect against perceived loss. Your job is to make "staying" feel as risky as "changing."

**Visual direction:**
- Background: #0a0a0a
- Two-path fork: "Change" path has a small risk flag in #ff4444 AND a large opportunity flag in #39d353 — prospect sees only the risk. "Stay" path has a comfort icon but also a hidden drain icon in #ff4444 (the cost of inaction)
- Arrow pointing to the hidden drain icon — "The cost they're not seeing"
- Body copy below at 16px in #c4b8e0

## Slide 5
**Headline:** The cost of inaction close.
**Body copy:** Instead of selling the solution, sell the cost of not having it. Calculate what inaction costs them per month — in lost revenue, wasted hours, missed quota, or churn. Once the number is real and on the table, staying put stops feeling like the safe choice.

**Word-for-word:** "Based on what you've shared, the gap you're describing is costing your team roughly [X] per month. What does that look like over a quarter?"

**Visual direction:**
- Background: #111111
- A growing red number on a ticker representing "monthly cost of inaction" — stylised clock/counter icon in #ff4444
- Script line in #f0ebff card with #814ac8 border
- "Make inaction expensive on paper." in #df7afe below the card

## Slide 6
**Headline:** Anchoring: the first number they hear shapes everything.
**Body copy:** Kahneman showed that the first number in any negotiation anchors all subsequent judgments. If your first number is too low, everything else feels expensive. If you anchor high and then move, the concession feels like a win. Always lead with the top of your range — then discount with a reason, not a plea.

**Visual direction:**
- Background: #0a0a0a
- Number line illustration: a horizontal bar — anchor point on the left ($$$), final price further right ($$)
- "Start here" label at the high anchor in #df7afe, "Land here" label at the final price in #39d353
- "Never anchor too low. You can't recover." in #c4b8e0 italic below
- Body copy at 16px

## Slide 7
**Headline:** The lesson: speak to loss, not gain.
**Body copy:** Reframe every pitch element in loss terms. Audit your deck. Audit your talk track. Wherever you say "you'll get" or "you'll gain" — ask whether it could be reframed as "right now you're losing." The same value lands 2x harder when it's framed as something they're already losing.
**Visual direction:**
- Background: #000000
- Before/after copy audit:
  "You'll gain 2 hours per rep per day." → crossed out
  "Right now you're losing 2 hours per rep per day." → underlined in #39d353
- Three examples in this format, stacked vertically
- Headline above at 32px in #f0ebff

## Slide 8
**Headline:** Your prospect isn't being difficult. They're being human.
**Body copy:** Loss aversion isn't irrational — it's the human brain doing its job. When you understand the psychology, the objection stops being frustrating and starts being informative. Every "I need to think about it" is just someone weighing a perceived loss. Your job is to help them see what they're already losing by standing still.
