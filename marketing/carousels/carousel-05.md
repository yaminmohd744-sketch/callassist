## Slide 2
**Headline:** The mistake: you're writing about you, not them.
**Body copy:** "Following up on our call." "Wanted to circle back." "Just checking in." — these are all about you. The prospect doesn't care that you're following up. They care if there's a reason to reply.

**Visual direction:**
- Dark card (#111111) listing the 3 dead phrases — each in #ff4444 with a small ✗ marker
- "They care if there's a reason to reply." in #df7afe below — the insight punch

---

## Slide 3
**Headline:** Part 1: Reference something they said.
**Body copy:** "You mentioned you're trying to hit Q3 targets — I've been thinking about the pipeline gap you described."

Start with THEIR words. It proves you were listening. That alone doubles your reply rate.

**Visual direction:**
- Quote highlight: the referenced phrase in a speech bubble (#814ac8 bg, #f0ebff text) — like quoting their own words back
- "That alone doubles your reply rate." in #39d353 below — reinforces the outcome

---

## Slide 4
**Headline:** Part 2: One specific value statement.
**Body copy:** "Based on what you described, I think the biggest lever for you is [X] — most teams in your situation see [outcome] within [timeframe]."

Not a feature list. Not your full deck. One thing, one outcome.

**Visual direction:**
- Clean card format — numbered "2" in large #814ac8 numeral top left
- Template text shown as a fill-in-the-blanks format — [X], [outcome], [timeframe] highlighted in #df7afe

---

## Slide 5
**Headline:** Part 3: Remove friction on the next step.
**Body copy:** "I have 15 minutes on [day] or [day] — which works? If neither fits, just tell me and I'll find something that does."

Give two options. Not "whenever works for you." Decisions kill momentum.

**Visual direction:**
- Calendar icon in #814ac8 top left of card
- "Two options, not open-ended." as the bottom takeaway in #df7afe italic

---

## Slide 6
**Headline:** Part 4: Keep it under 5 sentences.
**Body copy:** Long emails signal that you have too much time and too little respect for theirs. Every sentence that isn't earning its place is a reason to close the tab.

**Visual direction:**
- Visual: email body shown with many lines — most crossed out with a red strike, leaving only 5 clean lines
- The 5 remaining lines glow slightly in #f0ebff

---

## Slide 7
**Headline:** Part 5: Never say "just checking in."
**Body copy:** It's the most common close-killer in follow-up history. "Just" signals low status. "Checking in" signals you have no new value to offer. Delete it from your vocabulary permanently.

**Visual direction:**
- "Just checking in." text in a red-bordered tombstone style card — burial metaphor
- Crossed out, darkened
- "Delete it permanently." in #df7afe below with a checkmark
