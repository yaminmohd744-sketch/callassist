## Slide 2
**Headline:** Why objections are never what they sound like.
**Body copy:** An objection is a door that's slightly open. "Too expensive" is not a financial statement — it's an emotional one. The prospect is telling you they haven't yet connected your price to their pain. The objection is actually a request for more value. The reps who understand this never argue about price.
**Visual direction:**
- Background: #0a0a0a
- Headline: #df7afe ExtraBold, 36px
- "A request for more value." — isolated, #f0ebff, large bold — the reframe

## Slide 3
**Headline:** "It's too expensive."
**Body copy:** What they actually mean: "I don't see the value clearly enough yet." They're not comparing your price to their budget — they're comparing it to their current understanding of what they get. Your job isn't to lower the price. It's to raise the perceived value. Ask: "What would it need to do for the price to feel justified?"
**Visual direction:**
- Background: #111111
- Top section — objection as heard: large quote, #ff4444 tint, italic
- Middle section — actual meaning: #df7afe, translated label "WHAT THEY MEAN:" before it
- Bottom section — your move: white Regular, the question to ask
- Three-part structure per slide — creates a visual system across slides 3–7

## Slide 4
**Headline:** "I need to think about it."
**Body copy:** What they actually mean: "I'm not convinced — but I don't want conflict." This is the most common objection and the most avoided. "No problem, take your time" is the response that kills the deal slowly. Instead: "What specifically is making you hesitant? Is it the timing, the budget, or something about the fit?" Name the thing they're avoiding.
**Visual direction:**
- Background: #0a0a0a
- Same three-part format: objection (quoted, #ff4444 tint) → meaning (#df7afe) → your move (white)
- "Name the thing they're avoiding." — isolated line, bold, #f0ebff
- The contrast between the polite exit response and the direct follow-up should be palpable

---

## Slide 5
**Headline:** "Send me some information."
**Body copy:** What they actually mean: "I want to get off the phone without being rude." This is not an invitation — it's a polite hang-up. Do not send a PDF. Instead say: "Of course — what specific part would be most useful to focus on? That way I can make sure it's actually relevant to what you mentioned earlier." Make them define what they want.
**Visual direction:**
- Background: #111111
- Same three-part format
- "Do not send a PDF." — bold, #ff4444, slightly larger — blunt but necessary
- "Make them define what they want." — #df7afe, the tactical directive

## Slide 6
**Headline:** "We already have a solution."
**Body copy:** What they actually mean: "I don't understand what makes you different." They're not dismissing you — they're telling you they haven't seen your differentiation yet. Ask: "What does your current solution do well? And where does it fall short?" Let them name the gap. Then show them exactly that gap is where you live.
**Visual direction:**
- Background: #0a0a0a
- Same three-part format
- "Let them name the gap." — bolded, #df7afe
- The strategic elegance of letting the prospect surface their own dissatisfaction should be communicated

## Slide 7
**Headline:** "Now isn't a good time."
**Body copy:** What they actually mean: "This isn't a priority right now." Timing objections aren't about time — they're about urgency. The problem hasn't become painful enough to act on yet. Ask: "What would need to change in the next 90 days for this to become a priority?" You're not pushing — you're understanding what creates the trigger.
**Visual direction:**
- Background: #111111
- Same three-part format
- "Understanding what creates the trigger." — bolded, #df7afe — the strategic frame
- Calendar/clock icon faint in background at 5%, #814ac8

## Slide 8
**Headline:** The pattern underneath every objection.
**Body copy:** Every objection has the same structure: they're protecting themselves from a decision they haven't yet justified. Your job is never to argue, dismiss, or bulldoze the objection. It's to understand what they need to feel safe making the decision. Discovery doesn't end at the pitch. It continues through every objection.
**Visual direction:**
- Background: #0a0a0a
- Headline: #f0ebff ExtraBold, 36px
- "Understand what they need to feel safe making the decision." — bolded, #df7afe, the master principle
- "Discovery doesn't end at the pitch." — italic, isolated, white — the insight that separates closers from non-closers
- Body copy: white Regular

## Slide 9
**Headline:** The one question that handles almost every objection.
**Body copy:** "Help me understand — what's making you hesitant about moving forward?" It's open. It's non-confrontational. It gives them full permission to tell you the real thing. And the real thing is almost always something you can address. The close was right there — on the other side of the question.
